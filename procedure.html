Procedure

The Process of Donating Blood

The most common procedure that is carried out at the Donation Area is whole blood collection. We can obtain most products from the whole blood which is taken by donor. Here, we give a questionnaire to read beforehand in order to prepare themselves for the medical interview. If the donor thinks that there is nothing in the questionnaire that prohibits him/her from donating blood, the donor is registered in the Blood Bank. Donor fills the form which is given by employee of Blood bank. After the donor is instructed to start drinking water at reception.

A blood bank nurse will ask you some questions about your health, lifestyle, and disease risk factors. All of this information is confidential. Then we will take your pulse, temperature and blood pressure and haemoglobin level. A drop of blood from your finger will also be tested to ensure that your blood iron level is sufficient for you to donate. All medical equipment used for this test, as well as during the donation process, is sterile, used only once and then disposed. Before one enters the donation area, the donor is offered water. A donor who has been without food for some hours is advised to have a light snack, like biscuits and coffee , before donating blood. This would prevent unwanted effects like dizziness weakness during the blood donation procedure.

The components of blood include red blood cells and white blood cells, and cell fragments called platelets along with plasma which is liquid portion, that contains water, proteins, salts, lipids, and glucose.

Before Blood donation you have to:

  1. Drink an extra 16 ounces (473 millilitres) of water and other fluids before the donation.
  2. Eat a healthy meal before your donation. Avoid fatty foods;
  3. Wear something comfortable that can be rolled up above the elbow.
  4. If you have diabetes, blood pressure then bring a list of your current medications.
  5. Bring your ID (license, passport)
  6. Take plenty of sleep the night before you plan to donate.
  7. If you are a platelet donor, remember that you must not take aspirin for two days prior to donating. Otherwise, you can take your normal medications as prescribed.
  8. Tell the nurse about any prescription and/or over the counter medications that may be in your system.

During blood donation process...

The donor proceeds to the donation area were donor can lie comfortably on a couch, loosens any tight garments, like belts. The nurse explains the procedure (especially to first time donors) and proceeds to performs skin disinfection at the vane puncture site. The actual donation should be finished within 5 to 10 minutes. Any strange sensations should be reported immediately to the nurse. The donor is also monitored for any abnormal gestures during donation or any signs of fainting. When the blood donation is over, the donor is asked to press gently on the vane puncture site for some minutes.

Cosidering above estimation we can conclude that Donation of Blood is Need for the future of wellbeing.

After blood donation process...

After the donation, the donor is offered other refreshments like coffee or Tea and biscuits. A brief assessment is carried out by the nurses to confirm that the donor is feeling well and after about 15 minutes the donor may leave. Female donors may donate again after 4 months, while male donors may donate after 3 months. After donating you sit in an observation area, where you rest and eat a light snack. After 15 minutes, you can leave.

After Blood donation you have to:

  1. Drink extra fluids for the next day or two.
  2. Avoid strenuous physical activity or heavy lifting for the next five hours.
  3. If you feel lightheaded, lie down with your feet up until the feeling passes.
  4. Keep the bandage on your arm and dry for five hours..
  5. If you have bleeding after removing the bandage, put pressure on the site and raise your arm until the bleeding stops.
  6. If bleeding or bruising occurs under the skin, apply a cold pack to the area periodically during the first 24 hours.
  7. If your arm is sore, take a pain reliever such as acetaminophen (Tylenol, others). Avoid taking aspirin or ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin IB, others) for the first 24 to 48 hours after donation.
  8. Tell the nurse about any prescription and/or over the counter medications that may be in your system.

Blood Distribution

  1. The relatives of blood bank arrives at blood bank for collection of blood or we provide our courier service to receive blood sample from hospital.
  2. Physicians determine whether the patient requires a transfusion and, if so, which type.
  3. Blood transfusions are given to patients in a wide range of circumstances, including serious injuries (such as in a car crash) surgeries, child birth, anemia, blood disorders, cancer treatments, and many others.
  4. A patient suffering from an iron deficiency or anemia may receive red blood cells to increase their hemoglobin and iron levels, improving the amount of oxygen in the body.
  5. Patients who are unable to make enough platelets, due to illness or chemotherapy, may receive platelet transfusions to stay healthy.
  6. Plasma transfusions are used for patients with liver failure, severe infections, and serious burns.

What happens to my blood after a donation.

During a blood donation 475 ML of whole blood are drawn into the collection bag. This amount to about one twelfth of the total blood volume in a 70 kg person. Beginning of the blood donation some blood is collected in the sample pouch which is attached to the kit from which some sample bottles are filled. These sample bottles are to be used for screening of infectious disease markers and blood grouping tests and antibodies.

For confidentiality and traceability reasons all these samples are given a barcode number, so that once the samples are transferred to the Processing Laboratories, personnel cannot identify the donor. One unit of donated blood may be divided into components, including red cell concentrates, fresh frozen plasma, cryoprecipitate and platelet concentrates, to meet the needs of more than one patient. Red cells are stored in refrigerators at 6ºC for up to 42 days. Platelets are stored at room temperature in agitators for up to five days. Plasma and cryo are frozen and stored in freezers for up to one year.

Blood Testing

Test tubes arrive at a testing laboratory. A dozen tests are performed, to establish the blood type and test for infectious diseases like HIV 1 & 2, Hepatitis B and C. These tests are carried out to ensure that the patient receives the safest possible blood product. If a test result is positive, the donation will be discarded and will be notified (Test results are confidential and are only shared with the donor, except as may be required by law). When test results are received, units suitable for transfusion are labelled and stored.

A blood grouping test for ABO and RhD is also carried out with each blood donation. This is to ensure the correct blood groups for the donor, as the ABO and RhD blood groups have the potential to cause severe haemolysis (break down of red cells in the recipient) and/ or haemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN). In HDN the mother produces antibodies after being exposed to an antigen she does not have. These antibodies then cross the placenta to break down the baby’s red cells.

Some donations are also tested for cytomegalovirus (CMV) antibodies. This is done as some patients (but not all) require blood which is negative for CMV antibodies.

Blood Process at Blood Products

Freshly drawn whole blood maintains all its properties for a limited period of time. After a whole blood donation, blood is separated into different components. Whole blood is collected into the first bag of a 4-bag system. At the Processing Laboratory, the rest of the bags in the bag system are used during separation of whole blood into the various components.

  • The process of blood separation of a whole blood donation.

  • Step 1: Blood drawn from a donor.
  • Step 2: Whole blood donation.
  • Step 3: Blood is being filtered.
  • Step 4: Blood is being ready for centrifugation
  • Step 5: Separation of blood after centrifugation.
  • Step 6: Blood products after seperation.
  • Step 7: The ending products.
  • Step 8: Red cells are stored in a fridge between 20C to 60C for 42 days.
  • Step 9: Plasma kept in freezers at temp of -250C or lower for 36 months.
  • Step 10: Plateletes are stored in gas-permeable plastic bag at a temp of 200C to 240C for availability of oxygen to platelets.
